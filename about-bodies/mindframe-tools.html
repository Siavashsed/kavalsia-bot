Tool 01
Implementation Intention Builder
Gollwitzer (1999) · Meta-analytic effect size d = 0.65

Implementation intentions are if-then plans that specify when, where, and how you will act on a goal. Research by Peter Gollwitzer and colleagues shows they roughly double the rate of goal follow-through compared to vague intention alone. The mechanism is prospective memory: you pre-commit the action to an existing environmental cue, so less deliberate attention is needed to start.

    Why this works: the research
    • Implementation intentions outsource the initiation decision to the environment. When the situational cue appears (the anchor), the response is triggered semi-automatically, reducing reliance on motivation at the moment of action.
    • The strongest effect sizes appear when the new behaviour is attached to a cue that is unambiguous, frequent, and already well-established. Vague anchors like "when I have time" show much weaker effects.
    • Specificity is the mechanism. "After I pour my coffee, I will open my book" outperforms "I will read more" in goal attainment because the former delegates the decision to a perceptual event rather than a felt intention.
    Tool 02
    Cognitive Distortion Identifier
    Burns (1980) · CBT standard; Beck et al. (1979)

    Cognitive distortions are systematic errors in thinking that amplify negative emotion. Aaron Beck first mapped them in the 1960s; David Burns later named and popularised them. This tool helps you identify which patterns are active in a specific thought, then offers a structured reframe drawn from standard cognitive behavioural therapy technique.

    Research context
    • Cognitive restructuring - identifying and challenging distorted thoughts - is one of the most evidence-supported components of CBT. Meta-analyses consistently show effect sizes of d = 0.82-1.2 for CBT in depression (Cuijpers et al., 2019), though disentangling individual components is difficult.
    • The act of labelling a thought as a cognitive distortion creates psychological distance from it. This is the mechanism behind defusion techniques in Acceptance and Commitment Therapy as well, where the goal is not to eliminate the thought but to change your relationship to it.
    • CBT works best with a trained therapist. This tool is a practice aid, not a replacement. Noticing the pattern is step one; disputing it effectively often requires repeated practice and, for persistent low mood, professional guidance.
    Tool 03
    Sleep and Study Planner
    Cepeda et al. (2008) on spacing · Walker and Stickgold (2004) on sleep consolidation

    Sleep does active memory work. Studies by Matthew Walker, Robert Stickgold, and colleagues show that declarative memory consolidation - the transfer from hippocampal to cortical storage - occurs primarily during non-REM slow-wave sleep in the first half of the night. The spacing effect (Cepeda et al., 2008) compounds this: distributing study across days with sleep between sessions produces far more durable retention than massed practice.

    The science behind this planner
    • Sleep architecture cycles through roughly 90-minute cycles. A full night of 7-9 hours includes 4-5 cycles, with slow-wave sleep (key for declarative memory) concentrated in cycles 1-2, and REM (key for procedural and emotional memory) concentrated in cycles 3-5 toward morning.
    • Studying shortly before sleep means the material enters consolidation processing quickly. Studying too late can elevate cortisol and body temperature, delaying sleep onset and compressing the early slow-wave cycles.
    • The spacing effect is one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology. A 24-48 hour gap between study sessions - with at least one night of sleep between them - consistently outperforms equivalent time spent in a single massed session, especially for long-term retention beyond a week.
