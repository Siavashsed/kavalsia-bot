Canadian Market Watch

What the major markets are telling sellers right now.

A plain-language read on the cities that move the national numbers, written for the person trying to decide whether to list this season or wait. Less chart-staring, more "so what does this mean for my sale."

All figures below are illustrative ranges, refreshed seasonally to reflect the general shape of each market. They are not live quotes or appraisals. For your exact street, pull recent sold comparables on realtor.ca, HouseSigma, or Wahi, and talk to a local agent before you price.

The market snapshots

Each card reads the temperature, the rough numbers, and the one thing a seller in that market should actually do about it.

Toronto and the GTA
Ontario
Balanced, segmented
$1.0M to $1.2Mtypical detached range
$650K to $750Ktypical condo range

The GTA is really two markets. Freehold houses in good catchments still draw competition, while the condo segment is heavier with supply and slower to move. Land transfer tax stacks municipal plus provincial here, so buyers are unusually price-sensitive.

Seller takeaway: if you own a house, price at market and let competition work. If you own a condo, price sharp and stage hard; you are competing with a wall of similar units.
Vancouver and Lower Mainland
British Columbia
Pricey, supply-starved
$1.8M to $2.2Mtypical detached range
$750K to $900Ktypical condo range

The most expensive market in the country, and chronically short on listings. Detached supply is so tight that well-presented homes in desirable neighbourhoods still see strong interest. Buyers are stretched, so financing conditions and appraisal gaps come up more often here.

Seller takeaway: presentation pays off more here than almost anywhere. Weigh a clean offer with a solid deposit over a higher one leaning on financing, given how stretched buyers are.
Calgary
Alberta
Active, in-demand
$600K to $720Ktypical detached range
$300K to $360Ktypical apartment range

Strong interprovincial migration has kept Calgary one of the busier markets relative to its price point. No land transfer tax in Alberta is a real draw for buyers, and affordability compared to Toronto and Vancouver keeps demand healthy across most segments.

Seller takeaway: demand is on your side, but do not get greedy on price. Buyers moving from pricier provinces are savvy and will pass on an overpriced listing fast.
Ottawa
Ontario
Steady, stable
$700K to $820Ktypical detached range
$420K to $500Ktypical condo range

A government-anchored economy makes Ottawa one of the most even-keeled markets in the country. Fewer wild swings, fewer bidding frenzies, and buyers who tend to be deliberate. Family homes near good schools and transit hold their value reliably.

Seller takeaway: this is a price-it-right market, not a price-it-low-and-pray one. Underpricing rarely sparks a war here, so set a fair number and present cleanly.
Montreal
Quebec
Resilient, value-driven
$550K to $680Ktypical single-family range
$400K to $480Ktypical condo range

Still more affordable than Toronto or Vancouver, with steady demand and a strong rental and plex market. One Quebec quirk worth flagging: a notary, not a lawyer, handles the closing, and the welcome tax (the transfer duty) is a buyer cost that shapes their budget.

Seller takeaway: duplexes and triplexes attract investors as well as families. If you own a plex, market the rental income clearly; it widens your buyer pool.
Reading any market
The national signals
The numbers that matter
Months of supplyunder 4 favours sellers
Sale-to-listover 100% means competition

You do not need to follow every headline. Two numbers tell you most of the story: months of inventory (how long it would take to sell everything listed at the current pace) and the sale-to-list price ratio. Together they reveal who has the leverage.

Seller takeaway: low supply plus sales closing at or above asking means you can price confidently. Rising supply and softening ratios mean price sharp and move quickly.

Three rules that hold in every market

Conditions change by city and by season. These do not.

The first two weeks decide it

Buyer attention peaks the moment you list. Be priced right and show-ready on day one. The home that sits is the home that sells for less, in any market.

Interest rates set the ceiling

What buyers can offer is capped by what they can borrow. When rates ease, budgets stretch and competition rises. When they climb, buyers get cautious and conditional. Watch the rate, not just the price.

Local beats national, always

A national average is a blended number that may describe nobody's street. Your neighbourhood, your school catchment, and your home's condition matter far more than the headline. Always pull your own sold comparables.
