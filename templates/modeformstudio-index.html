Chapter One

Designers in studio.

A standing invitation to enter the room where a collection is decided - the toiles, the half-finished thoughts, the music nobody admits to.

Atelier Visit / Paris 8e

The patternmaker keeps her own quiet hours.

On a wet Tuesday in February, we sit with Sigrid Bjornsen at a long oak table covered in muslin. She is, by her own account, three weeks late on a coat and entirely unbothered. The studio smells of bread from the bakery downstairs and the very specific dust of cotton calico.

What follows is the slowest interview we have ever published. We talk about a single shoulder seam for forty minutes. We argue, gently, about the politics of cutting on the bias. It is, in our view, the only honest way to write about clothes.

Chapter Two

From the studio floor.

A working studio is a kind of argument in slow motion.

We spent the better part of a season inside three working studios - one in Antwerp, one in a converted laundry in east London, one above a fishmonger in Naples. None of them looked like a magazine. All of them taught us something about the difference between fashion and clothing.

"You don't design a coat. You argue with one until it finally lets you sleep."

This is the first in a quarterly series. Future visits include a millinery in Lisbon, an ateliers de broderie outside Lyon, and a single, stubborn knitter in the Outer Hebrides.

Field reporting / Lila Vandermeer · Spring 2026

Chapter Four

The Process.

Seven questions we are most often asked by designers, agencies and readers thinking about subscribing. Answered plainly, in the order they tend to arrive.

A quarterly issue plus a weekly studio dispatch. Each issue holds five to seven long essays, a casting board, and one closely reported studio visit. We do not do trend pieces. We do not do shopping guides.
Lila Vandermeer is the founding editor and casting director. A small masthead of five contributors covers photography, design criticism, studio reporting and art direction. We commission outside writers rarely and credit them clearly.
No. The studio is reader-supported through subscriptions and a small annual print issue. We accept no PR trips, no gifted garments, and no sponsored coverage. Editorial decisions are made before we ask anyone for access.
We work from our own polaroid board, built over years of open castings in Paris, London, Copenhagen, Tokyo and New York. Agencies are credited. Bookings are made by working casting directors, not by an algorithm.
Yes, and we read every note. We tend to choose ateliers that have been making work for at least three seasons, that own their patternmaking, and that are willing to let us sit quietly for a full day without staging anything.
Only when it changes the clothes. We are interested in how a fabric is sourced, how a sample is paid for, how a small label survives a slow season. We are not interested in quarterly earnings calls.
One per year, hand-bound in a run of nine hundred. Issue 14 closes for print on June 30. Subscribers are notified first; remaining copies are sold through three independent bookshops in Antwerp, Tokyo and New York.
