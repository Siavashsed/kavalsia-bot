Service Interval - FAQ

The questions that come up.

01How often should a modern mechanical watch be serviced?
+
The honest answer the brands will not give you: when the timing drifts, not when the calendar says so. A modern caliber with synthetic lubricants and a free-sprung balance can run 7-10 years between full services if it is worn regularly and not exposed to magnetism. The 4-year interval most brands quote is a revenue model, not an engineering recommendation.
02In-house movement vs ETA/Sellita - does it actually matter?
+
For reliability, no. A well-finished Sellita SW300 will outlast many in-house calibers. For collectability and finishing potential, yes - in-house calibers leave room for hand work that mass-produced ebauches do not. Pay for the finishing, not for the marketing label.
03Why does Caliber Notes not run sponsored brand placements?
+
Because brand access compromises the loupe. The moment a maker provides loaner watches, factory tours, and dinner in Geneva, the writing softens. We pay for our own loupes, our own travel, and our own movements. It costs more. It reads truer.
04What is a fair price ceiling for a daily wear mechanical?
+
Anything above $8,000 is paying for finishing, name, and resale - not function. A 1995 Grand Seiko 9F or a modern Tudor with an MT5612 will keep time within COSC tolerance for decades. The watches we cover above that ceiling are covered for what they are, not because they tell time better.
05Which timing-machine readings actually matter?
+
Amplitude first, then beat error, then rate. A movement at +5 s/day with 275° amplitude is healthier than one running on time at 220°. Anything under 250° dial-up after a full wind means the train needs attention, regardless of what the seconds hand is doing.
06Are silicon hairsprings a good thing?
+
Mechanically, yes - antimagnetic, temperature-stable, geometrically precise. Philosophically, it depends what you want from a mechanical watch. If you want a precision instrument, silicon wins. If you want a piece of three-hundred-year-old craft on your wrist, the answer is more complicated.
